case study ii

Operator Performance in Automated Control Rooms

Postdoctoral Researcher & Research Program Lead · University of Toronto
Goal
Produce the empirical evidence base needed to support Canadian regulatory decision-making on operator performance in next-generation automated nuclear control rooms.
My Role
Directed the experimental program: defined research questions, designed studies, managed an 8-person team of junior researchers and engineers, and delivered findings to the regulator.
Stakeholders
Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission, Idaho National Laboratory, Cognitive Engineering Laboratory at the University of Toronto.
Constraints
Emerging reactor type with no operational precedent. No existing human factors guidance for multi-unit automated control rooms. Findings needed to withstand regulatory scrutiny.
Context

A new operating concept with no human factors evidence

Small modular reactors represent a fundamental shift in how nuclear plants are operated. Unlike conventional reactors — where a dedicated crew monitors a single unit — SMR designs envision a single operator supervising multiple reactor units simultaneously from a shared digital control room. Automation handles routine control. The operator's role shifts from active controller to supervisory monitor.

This shift from active control to supervisory monitoring is one of the most studied problems in human factors. It introduces well-documented risks: loss of situational awareness, complacency, skill degradation, and difficulty resuming manual control when automation fails.

The Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission identified this as a critical gap. Before licensing any SMR design for operation in Canada, the regulator needed evidence that operators could maintain safe performance under these new conditions. No such evidence existed. My job was to build the research program that produced it.

Research Design

Structuring the program around four open questions

I scoped the program around the four questions the regulator needed answered — each one became a workstream with its own series of experiments, but all shared a common simulator environment and measurement framework.

  • How do you measure operator performance in a digital control room where the operator is primarily monitoring rather than actively controlling? Traditional performance metrics assume hands-on control. We needed new measures.
  • What happens to situational awareness and workload when a single operator supervises multiple reactor units simultaneously? At what point does cognitive demand exceed safe capacity?
  • Does automated decision-aiding actually help? Automation is meant to support operators, but it can also introduce complacency, miscalibrated trust, and new failure modes. Under what conditions does it improve performance, and when does it degrade it?
  • How should overview displays be designed so that operators can rapidly assess system state across multiple units? What information architecture supports fast, accurate situation assessment?
Framing the program around decision-relevant questions — rather than around methods or technologies — ensured every study produced evidence the regulator could act on.
Experimental Environment

A microworld for controlled operator-in-the-loop testing

All experiments were conducted in the Rancor microworld — a research simulator designed to replicate the supervisory control demands of a multi-unit SMR control room under controlled conditions. Rancor presents operators with realistic plant dynamics, alarm systems, and automated support tools while allowing precise manipulation of experimental variables.

The simulator enabled us to do what field observation cannot: systematically vary automation levels, unit count, fault severity, and display configurations while holding everything else constant. This was essential for isolating the causal effects of specific design choices on operator performance.

Methods

Techniques applied across the program

Technique Application
Controlled experiments Within- and between-subjects designs manipulating automation level, unit count, fault type, and display configuration
Situational awareness measurement Probe-based and freeze-based SA assessment during simulated operations to quantify operator comprehension of system state
Workload assessment Subjective (NASA-TLX) and physiological measures to evaluate cognitive demand under varying operational conditions
Behavioral data collection Response time, detection accuracy, control actions, and error rates logged throughout simulated scenarios
Statistical modeling ANOVA, regression, and multilevel modeling in Python and SPSS to analyze performance data and identify significant effects
Literature review & framework development Systematic review of human-automation interaction research to build theoretical foundations for study design and interpretation
Team Leadership

Directing an 8-person research team

I led a team of eight junior researchers and engineers across the program. My responsibilities included defining research questions and hypotheses, designing experimental protocols, assigning workstream ownership, reviewing analysis work, and ensuring methodological consistency across studies.

Managing a research team in an academic setting means balancing scientific rigor with the developmental needs of junior researchers — each study is both a research deliverable and a training opportunity.

I also managed the practical logistics of running human-subjects experiments: participant recruitment, ethics board protocols, simulator scheduling, data management, and quality control. Each study involved multiple sessions with trained participants performing realistic operator tasks under controlled conditions.

Key Findings

What the experiments revealed

The program produced a body of evidence that directly addressed the regulator's questions. Three findings were particularly consequential for design and policy.

Multi-unit monitoring degrades situational awareness in predictable ways. As operators supervised more units simultaneously, their ability to accurately assess system state declined — but the decline was not uniform. Operators maintained awareness of the unit they were actively attending to while losing track of background units. This had direct implications for alarm design and display prioritization.

Automated decision-aiding improved performance under normal conditions but introduced new risks during automation failures. When automation worked as expected, operators responded faster and more accurately. But when automation provided incorrect guidance or failed silently, operators who had been relying on it were slower to detect the problem and more likely to follow the faulty recommendation. This finding shaped recommendations on automation transparency and operator override design.

The automation trust finding is consistent with decades of research on automation complacency — but demonstrating it empirically in an SMR-specific context gave the regulator concrete evidence to act on, rather than relying on general principles.

Overview display design significantly affected fault detection time. Operators using displays designed around functional groupings detected cross-unit faults faster than those using displays organized by unit. This finding directly informed recommendations on information architecture for SMR control room displays.

Dissemination

From lab findings to regulatory guidance

The program produced 15+ scientific and technical publications presented at international conferences and published in peer-reviewed journals. But the primary audience was the Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission. Findings were delivered through technical reports structured to support regulatory decision-making — not just presenting results, but framing their implications for specific design and licensing questions.

This required translating experimental evidence into language and formats that regulatory reviewers could use: clear statements of what the data supports, what it does not, and what the implications are for operator staffing, automation requirements, and display design standards in future SMR control rooms.

Outcome & Impact

What this program delivered

The research program produced the first empirical evidence base for human factors in multi-unit automated nuclear control rooms. The findings directly informed the Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission's regulatory approach to SMR human factors, addressing questions that had no prior evidence base.
