Human Factors Research Program: SMR Multi-Unit Operations

Postdoctoral Researcher & Team Lead of Experimentation · Cognitive Engineering Lab, University of Toronto
Goal
Verify and validate main control room designs for a new-build nuclear reactor, ensuring operator interfaces support safe, reliable human performance.
My Role
Human factors analysis, cognitive task analysis, workload assessment, heuristic evaluation of HMI, human reliability inputs.
Team
Multidisciplinary — HFE analysts, systems engineers, I&C engineers, operations staff, reactor vendor.
Constraints
Nuclear regulatory compliance (CNSC). Safety-critical domain — errors in design directly threaten plant and public safety.
Problem

Where we started

Nuclear power plant operators manage thousands of alarms, indicators, and controls from a main control room. These interfaces mediate every safety-critical decision the operator makes — during normal operations, anticipated transients, and emergency conditions.

The challenge was not a redesign of an existing system but the verification of a new-build control room: confirming that the proposed HMI designs, alarm systems, display hierarchies, and workstation layouts would actually support human performance before the plant was built and operational. Getting this wrong is not a usability issue — it is a safety issue with regulatory consequences.

In nuclear HFE, the verification program must demonstrate compliance before construction. There is no "launch and iterate" — the design must be right the first time.
Research & Analysis

Understanding operator needs and task demands

The first step was understanding what operators actually need to do — and under what conditions. I conducted cognitive task analyses to map decision-making workflows across plant states: normal steady-state operations, anticipated operational occurrences, and design-basis emergency scenarios.

This involved working closely with operations staff and systems engineers to decompose tasks into decision points, information requirements, and action sequences. The goal was not to document what operators should do in theory, but what the interface needed to support in practice — including high-workload situations where attention is divided and time pressure is real.

Cognitive task analysis captures what isn't visible in procedures: the mental models, cue-response patterns, and situation awareness demands that drive operator performance.

In parallel, I performed workload assessments to identify scenarios where operator capacity could become a bottleneck — particularly during multi-unit events or concurrent alarms requiring action across multiple workstations.

Evaluation

Reviewing designs against human factors criteria

With the task demands mapped, I conducted systematic heuristic evaluations of the proposed control room interfaces — structured around NUREG-0700 guidance covering display layout, navigation, labeling, color coding, and alarm presentation.

This was not a checklist exercise. Each evaluation required judgment about whether a specific design choice would support or hinder operator performance in the context of the tasks identified in the analysis phase. Where issues were found, I documented them with specific references to the task context and the regulatory criterion, and proposed design alternatives.

Heuristic evaluation in nuclear HFE is standards-based but context-dependent — the same display element may be acceptable for one task and problematic for another.

The alarm system received particular attention. I evaluated alarm prioritization, grouping, and presentation logic to ensure operators would not be overwhelmed during plant transients — a known failure mode in control room design that has contributed to incidents in the industry.

Collaboration & Iteration

Working across disciplines to resolve design issues

HFE findings don't exist in isolation. Each issue intersects with systems engineering constraints, I&C architecture, and operational procedures. I participated in multidisciplinary design review sessions where human factors findings were weighed against technical feasibility and regulatory requirements.

This meant advocating for operator-centered design within an engineering-driven process — explaining why a particular display layout or alarm grouping was problematic in human performance terms, and working with the design team to find solutions that satisfied both human factors criteria and systems engineering constraints.

In safety-critical domains, the HFE analyst is often the only voice in the room representing the operator's cognitive and perceptual limitations. Making that case compellingly to engineers is a core skill.
Risk Integration

Connecting human factors to safety analysis

Beyond interface evaluation, I contributed human reliability analysis inputs to the plant's probabilistic safety assessment. This involved estimating human error probabilities for safety-critical operator actions — quantifying the likelihood that an operator would fail to respond correctly under specific conditions.

HRA bridges human factors and nuclear safety analysis: it translates qualitative findings about interface quality, workload, and procedure design into quantitative inputs that feed directly into the plant's safety case. The results influenced both interface design decisions and the overall risk profile of the reactor.

Outcome & Impact

What this work contributed

The HFE verification program identified design issues across alarm management, display navigation, and workstation layout that were resolved before detailed design finalization. The human reliability analysis inputs directly informed the plant's probabilistic safety case.
